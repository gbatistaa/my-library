# MyLibrary B2B2C Execution Plan

## Goal

Take MyLibrary from a strong product idea to a sellable B2B2C SaaS for reading communities, with a clear path to the first paying customer.

This document is intentionally practical.
It is designed to answer:

- who pays
- why they pay
- what they buy first
- how pricing should work
- how to get the first paying accounts in 90 days

## Commercial Positioning

**MyLibrary helps book clubs and reading communities increase participation through streaks, challenges, leaderboards, and measurable reading progress.**

This is the core commercial promise.

Do not lead with:

- personal library organization
- book cataloging
- shelf aesthetics
- Goodreads replacement

Lead with:

- engagement
- retention
- challenge infrastructure
- community identity
- proof of participation

## ICP

### Primary ICP

**Paid or semi-paid reading communities with one clear operator.**

Profile:

- 30 to 500 members
- one founder, creator, moderator, or admin making decisions
- already runs monthly reads, challenges, sprints, or discussions
- currently manages engagement manually in Discord, WhatsApp, Telegram, Notion, or spreadsheets
- cares about churn, participation, and perceived value

Examples:

- BookTok creator with a paid club
- newsletter creator with a reading membership
- Discord-based book club with monthly subscription
- private reading cohort run by a host or educator

Why this ICP first:

- fast decisions
- obvious pain
- low procurement friction
- direct ROI language
- founder can sell directly

### Secondary ICP

**Independent education and literacy programs.**

Profile:

- reading coaches
- after-school programs
- small private schools
- language-learning communities using books
- independent libraries with digital engagement programs

Why second:

- still need participation and reporting
- stronger operational needs
- slower sales than creators, but higher trust value

### ICP To Avoid At First

- public school systems
- large library networks
- publishers
- universities
- enterprise HR wellness programs

Reason:

- longer sales cycles
- procurement complexity
- compliance burden
- integration expectations too early

## Problem Statement By Buyer

### What The Organizer Feels

- "My members join, then disappear."
- "Engagement depends too much on manual reminders."
- "I have no simple way to show who is active."
- "Challenges are hard to run consistently."
- "People need visible progress and recognition."
- "I need my community to feel alive enough to justify the subscription price."

### What The Member Feels

- "I want motivation to keep reading."
- "I like seeing my progress."
- "I want recognition."
- "I want a reason to come back."
- "I want to feel part of something."

Your product must solve both sides, but the payer is the organizer.

## Offer Design

### Core Offer

MyLibrary is the engagement layer for reading communities.

The first paid offer should include:

- private club space
- member onboarding
- reading session tracking
- streaks and goals
- achievements
- group leaderboard
- monthly challenge support
- basic community analytics
- shareable progress assets

### Why This Offer Sells

Because it replaces manual community operations:

- spreadsheet tracking
- ad hoc ranking
- reminder chaos
- no visibility into activity
- weak gamification
- weak proof of value

## Product Packaging

### Member Experience

- create profile
- join club by invite or code
- add books
- log reading sessions
- maintain streak
- hit goals
- earn achievements
- climb leaderboard
- share progress

### Organizer Experience

- create club
- brand club
- invite members
- launch reading challenge
- track activity by member
- view participation summary
- see leaderboard by period
- highlight milestones publicly

If the organizer cannot do those things in a clean flow, the B2B2C strategy breaks.

## Pricing

## Pricing Principles

- charge for admin value, not basic reading actions
- make adoption easy for small communities
- use member count and admin features as upgrade triggers
- keep the first paid plan cheap enough to buy without a committee
- reserve customization and multi-club controls for higher tiers

## Recommended Pricing

Use local pricing if selling first in Brazil.
If selling globally, keep a USD equivalent.

### Free: R$ 0 / month

- 1 club
- up to 20 members
- book and session tracking
- streaks, goals, achievements
- basic leaderboard
- limited challenge templates
- limited analytics window
- MyLibrary branding

Purpose:

- product adoption
- low-friction pilots
- creator experimentation

### Pro: R$ 149 / month

- 1 club
- up to 150 members
- unlimited challenges
- richer leaderboard filters
- club analytics dashboard
- automated reminders
- shareable progress cards
- branded club page basics
- admin export CSV

This should be the main plan.

It is cheap enough for creators and private clubs, but expensive enough to validate business value.

### Scale: R$ 399 / month

- up to 3 clubs
- up to 750 members total
- moderator roles
- seasonal competitions
- advanced analytics
- member activity segments
- custom achievement sets
- custom branding
- priority support

This plan targets serious communities and operators with multiple programs.

### Enterprise / Education: Starting at R$ 1,500 / month

- custom member limits
- onboarding support
- multi-admin governance
- reporting exports
- white-label or near-white-label options
- SLA and custom support

Only sell this after the product is operationally stable.

## Pricing Notes

- If your first customers hesitate at `R$ 149`, you probably have not yet proved operational value.
- If they say yes easily, `R$ 149` is probably too low in the medium term.
- Do not start at `R$ 19` or `R$ 29`. That traps you in low-value positioning.

## Upgrade Triggers

Your paywall should be driven by:

- member count
- analytics depth
- number of active challenges
- automated reminders
- moderator/admin controls
- branding and custom achievements

Do not gate basic member actions.

## Revenue Scenarios

### Early Validation

- 10 Pro customers = `R$ 1,490 MRR`
- 20 Pro customers = `R$ 2,980 MRR`
- 10 Pro + 5 Scale = `R$ 3,485 MRR`

This is not huge, but it proves the model.

### Strong Small SaaS

- 50 Pro customers = `R$ 7,450 MRR`
- 20 Scale customers = `R$ 7,980 MRR`
- combined example = `R$ 15,430 MRR`

Now the business starts to look real.

### Real Growth Path

To make meaningful money, you likely need:

- 100+ Pro accounts
- 20+ Scale accounts
- some education or enterprise deals

That is where B2B2C becomes materially better than pure B2C subscription economics.

## Objection Handling

### "We already use Discord/WhatsApp."

Yes, but those tools do not provide structured progress, recognition, or measurable engagement.

### "Our members are casual readers."

That is exactly why visible progress and gentle competition matter.

### "This seems like extra work."

The product only wins if it reduces manual admin work and gives members a stronger reason to return.

## Sales Motion

### Stage 1: Founder-Led Sales

Use direct outreach and live demos.

Targets:

- BookTok creators
- Bookstagram creators
- Discord reading clubs
- paid newsletters with reading programs
- online reading coaches

Approach:

- short personalized message
- identify their current challenge structure
- show how MyLibrary makes engagement visible
- offer a pilot for one group

### Outreach Message Template

Hi, I built a platform for reading communities that want stronger participation without running everything manually in spreadsheets and chat threads.

It gives your members streaks, leaderboards, challenges, and visible reading progress inside one club space.

If you run monthly reads or challenges, I can show you in 15 minutes how this could increase participation inside your community.

### Demo Goal

The demo should prove only three things:

1. members have a strong habit loop
2. admins can see participation clearly
3. the community becomes easier to run

Do not overload the demo with every feature.

## First Customer Acquisition Strategy

### Target

Get **3 pilot communities** before trying to scale acquisition.

Ideal pilot profile:

- 30 to 100 members
- active organizer
- monthly reading activity already exists
- willing to give feedback
- willing to share results if positive

### Offer For Pilots

- free onboarding
- 30-day guided pilot
- direct founder support
- discounted first paid conversion

Pilot pricing suggestion:

- month 1 free
- month 2 at `R$ 79`
- full Pro after proven value

This is acceptable for initial validation.
Do not keep everyone permanently discounted.

## What Must Be True Before Charging

Before you ask for full money, the product should prove:

- members actually log sessions
- the leaderboard updates cleanly
- organizer can launch a challenge without friction
- analytics show useful participation signals
- members engage at least weekly

If those are not true, charging early will create churn and bad references.

## 90-Day Roadmap To First Paying Customer

## Days 1-15: Narrow The Product

Goal:

Define the minimal commercial version.

Must-have decisions:

- one target ICP only
- one clear onboarding flow for club admin
- one member activity loop
- one analytics dashboard for organizer
- one leaderboard format
- one shareable progress mechanic

Deliverables:

- product messaging draft
- pricing page draft
- demo environment
- landing page copy draft

## Days 16-30: Build The Organizer Core

Goal:

Make the product sellable to a club owner.

Priority features:

- create club
- invite members
- member join flow
- group leaderboard
- challenge configuration
- organizer dashboard with participation summary

Avoid:

- edge-case polish
- advanced customization
- peripheral personal features

## Days 31-45: Build The Habit Loop

Goal:

Make members come back.

Priority features:

- quick session logging
- streak visibility
- goal progress
- achievement feedback
- weekly leaderboard refresh
- social progress sharing

Success condition:

The member should understand progress and status in less than 30 seconds.

## Days 46-60: Pilot Readiness

Goal:

Prepare to put real communities inside.

Priority tasks:

- remove obvious debug friction
- tighten onboarding
- basic error handling
- basic admin analytics
- basic notification strategy
- create demo data and screenshots

Commercial tasks:

- compile lead list of 30 targets
- write outreach sequence
- book 10 demos

## Days 61-75: Pilot Execution

Goal:

Run pilots with 2 to 3 communities.

What to do:

- onboard communities manually
- watch usage closely
- talk to organizers weekly
- measure session logging, participation, and challenge usage
- fix blockers fast

Metrics to watch:

- weekly active members
- percent logging sessions
- streak adoption
- leaderboard views
- challenge participation

## Days 76-90: Convert To Paid

Goal:

Close the first paying customer.

Conversion criteria:

- organizer says the tool made the group easier to run
- visible participation increased
- members actually used the loop
- organizer wants the challenge infrastructure to continue

Close with:

- discounted first month if needed
- annual discount only if it helps cash flow
- request testimonial and referral after success

## First KPI Set

Track these from day one:

- number of active clubs
- number of weekly active members per club
- session logs per active member
- percent of members with streak greater than 1
- challenge participation rate
- admin weekly logins
- club retention after 30 days
- pilot to paid conversion rate

## Product Priorities

If resources are limited, build in this order:

1. club creation and member join
2. session logging
3. streak and goals
4. group leaderboard
5. organizer analytics
6. challenge management
7. progress sharing

If something lower on the list blocks something higher, it can move up.
Otherwise, stay disciplined.

## Features To Delay

Delay these until the business case is proven:

- deep customization
- complex saga workflows
- advanced social feed features
- white-label
- full institution reporting
- complex notification automations
- large integration surface

## Final Strategic Note

The highest-value version of MyLibrary is not "an app readers like."

It is:

**a system organizers rely on because it makes their reading community more active, more valuable, and easier to run.**

If you keep product decisions tied to that sentence, the business can become real.
If not, it risks becoming a well-built app with weak monetization.
